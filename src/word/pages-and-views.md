  <section class="section-light">
  <p>You may need to adjust how you display your document's page(s) while editing it. This article goes through the basics of zooming and altering page views in Word.</p> 

  <h2>View tab</h2>
  <p>Under the <strong>View</strong> tab you have several options to change how the document is displayed. It also features <span class="glossary-hover" data-term="zoom">zoom</span> tools that let you control the magnification of the document and whether to have one or more pages shown on screen.</p>
    <p><img class="thumbnail border" src="{{ '/assets/images/word/Pages and Views/View tab.png' | url }}"  alt="View tab on Ribbon in Word" style="max-width:700px; width:100%; height:auto; vertical-align:middle"></p>

  <h3>Zoom</h3>
   <p><img class="thumbnail border left" src="{{ '/assets/images/word/Pages and Views/Zoom on Ribbon.png' | url }}"  alt="View tab with zoom options on Ribbon in Word" style="max-width:250px; width:100%; height:auto; vertical-align:middle"></p>
  <p>The first option to talk about is <strong>Zoom</strong>. But instead of clicking on the button above within <strong>View > Zoom</strong>, let's look at the zoom scroller as it's quicker to use; it's found in the bottom right of the screen on the <strong>status bar</strong> discussed in the <a href="/word/getting-started-word/">Getting started with Word</a> article.</p>
  <p><img class="thumbnail border left" src="{{ '/assets/images/word/Pages and Views/Zoom slider.png' | url }}"  alt="Zoom slider on status bar in Word" style="max-width:200px; width:100%; height:auto; vertical-align:middle"></p>
  <p>There are other ways to zoom as well:</p>
  <ul>
      <li>If you're using a mouse, the fastest way, and <u>the way I'd recommend zooming</u> for ease, is to hold down <strong>Ctrl + slide the mouse wheel up</strong> to zoom in and <strong>Ctrl + slide the mouse wheel down</strong> to zoom out. If you don't have a mouse some laptop mousepads offer this function, normally down one side of the mousepad.</li>
  </ul>
  <p>Otherwise there's these keyboard shortcuts you can zoom with:</p>
  <ul>
      <li><strong>Ctrl and +</strong> to zoom in <img class="thumbnail left" src="{{ '/assets/images/keyboard shortcuts/Ctrl++.png' | url }}" alt="Ctrl and + shortcut: Zoom in"  style="max-width:200px; width:100%; height:auto; vertical-align:middle"></li>
      <li><strong>Ctrl and -</strong> to zoom out<img class="thumbnail left"  src="{{ '/assets/images/keyboard shortcuts/Ctrl+-.png' | url }}" alt="Ctrl and - shortcut: Zoom out"  style="max-width:200px; width:100%; height:auto; vertical-align:middle"></li>
      <li><strong>Ctrl + 0 (zero)</strong> will reset zoom level to 100%<img class="thumbnail left"  src="{{ '/assets/images/keyboard shortcuts/Ctrl+0.png' | url }}" alt="Ctrl + 0 shortcut: reset zoom level"  style="max-width:200px; width:100%; height:auto; vertical-align:middle"></li>
  </ul>


  <h3>Zoom options</h3>
  <p>You can find zoom options by clicking on the <strong>percentage</strong> on the right of the zoom scroller (found bottom right of the screen), or by clicking the <strong>Zoom</strong> button under <strong>View > Zoom</strong> shown above.</p>
  <img class="thumbnail border left" src="{{ '/assets/images/word/Pages and Views/Zoom options via slider.png' | url }}"  alt="Zoom options via zoom scroller in Word" style="max-width:225px; width:100%; height:auto; vertical-align:middle">
  <img class="thumbnail border left" src="{{ '/assets/images/word/Pages and Views/Zoom on Ribbon Zoom option.png' | url }}"  alt="View tab Zoom options on Ribbon in Word" style="max-width:250px; width:100%; height:auto; vertical-align:middle">



  <p>There are a range of zoom options letting you choose how to display the page(s). 100% is the standard zoom level for editing. Here you can choose between options to zoom via a <strong>percentage</strong> or to the <strong>page width</strong> or to see the <strong>whole page</strong>. I wouldn't recommend using <strong>Many pages</strong> unless you're looking for a page that is easily recognisable viewed alongside multiple pages. This option is explored in the <a href="?difficulty=intermediate">intermediate version</a> of this article if you want to understand it better. <strong>Text width</strong> as well is very zoomed in and not ideal for editing a document at length.</p>
  <p><img class="thumbnail border left" src="{{ '/assets/images/word/Pages and Views/Zoom.png' | url }}"  alt="Zoom tool options in Word" style="max-width:300px; width:100%; height:auto; vertical-align:middle"></p>

  <h3>One Page and Multiple Pages</h3>  
  <p>Under <strong>View > Zoom</strong> you can also choose whether to display a single page or multiple pages when you're zoomed out far enough.</p>
  <p><strong>One page view</strong>: Use this when you want to focus on one page at a time which you'll typically be doing. This is the default setting.</p>
  <p><img class="thumbnail border left" src="{{ '/assets/images/word/Pages and Views/One Page.png' | url }}"  alt="One page view in Word" style="max-width:500px; width:100%; height:auto; vertical-align:middle"></p>
  <p><strong>Multiple pages view</strong>: This lets you see multiple pages side by side when you zoom out far enough. When you zoom into a page it will snap to showing you just that page.</p>
  <p><img class="thumbnail border left" src="{{ '/assets/images/word/Pages and Views/Multiple Pages.png' | url }}"  alt="Multiple pages view in Word" style="max-width:500px; width:100%; height:auto; vertical-align:middle"></p>

  <h3 id="views">Views</h3>
    <p>Microsoft Word has different views which you can switch to in order to see your document content displayed differently for various purposes. The default view is called <span class="glossary-hover" data-term="print-layout">Print Layout</span>.</p>
  <h4 id="print-layout">Print Layout</h4>
    <p>This is the default view that shows how the document will look when printed. There is a white page against a grey background which will display layout elements like <span class="glossary-hover" data-term="margin">margins</span>, <span class="glossary-hover" data-term="header">headers</span> and <span class="glossary-hover" data-term="footer">footers</span>, images and <span class="glossary-hover" data-term="page-break">page breaks</span>.</p>
    <p><img class="thumbnail border"  src="{{ '/assets/images/word/Pages and Views/Print Layout.png' | url }}" alt="Print layout view in Microsoft Word"  style="max-width:700px; width:100%; height:auto; vertical-align:middle"></p>
  <h4>Other views</h4>
    <p>To explore other views like <span class="glossary-hover" data-term="read-mode">Read mode</span> and views for specific purposes like <span class="glossary-hover" data-term="outline-view">outline view</span>, these are explored in the <a href="?difficulty=intermediate#views-int">intermediate version</a> of this article.</p>

  <h2 id="layout-tab">Layout tab</h2>
  <p>Under <strong>Layout</strong> you can change the <strong>size</strong> and <strong>orientation</strong> of a page. You can also alter <strong>margins</strong>, <strong>columns</strong> and apply other tools like <strong>hyphenation</strong> page breaks and section breaks which are a little more advanced.</p>
  <p><img class="thumbnail border" src="{{ '/assets/images/word/Pages and Views/Layout Page Setup.png' | url }}"  alt="Layout tab options on ribbon in Word" style="max-width:350px; width:100%; height:auto; vertical-align:middle"></p>

  <p><strong>Size</strong> and <strong>Orientation</strong> are the most common tools you'll use here. These are explored more in the <a href="?difficulty=intermediate#layout-tab-int">intermediate version</a> of this article.</p>
  <p><img class="thumbnail" src="{{ '/assets/images/word/Pages and Views/Orientation and Size.png' | url }}"  alt="Page Orientation and Size options in Word" style="max-width:450px; width:100%; height:auto; vertical-align:middle"></p>

  <h3 id="views-int">Views</h3>
  <p>Microsoft Word has different views you can switch to in order to view your document content in different ways for various purposes. The default view is called <strong>Print Layout</strong>.</p>

  <h4 id="print-layout-int">Print Layout</h4>
  <p>This default view shows you how the document will look when printed. It features the page layout and layout elements such as <span class="glossary-hover" data-term="margin">margins</span>, the <span class="glossary-hover" data-term="header">headers</span> and <span class="glossary-hover" data-term="footer">footers</span>, images and <span class="glossary-hover" data-term="page-break">page breaks</span>. Above the page you have the <span class="glossary-hover" data-term="quick-access-toolbar">quick access toolbar</span> and <span class="glossary-hover" data-term="ribbon">ribbon</span> and below the status bar. All the tools are at your disposal in this view.</p>
  <p><img class="thumbnail border"  src="{{ '/assets/images/word/Pages and Views/Print Layout.png' | url }}" alt="Print layout view in Microsoft Word"  style="max-width:700px; width:100%; height:auto; vertical-align:middle"></p>
  <p>If you're in another view, Print layout can be found either on the <span class="glossary-hover" data-term="ribbon">ribbon</span> via <strong>View > Views</strong> or via the Print layout button on the <span class="glossary-hover" data-term="status-bar">status bar</span> at the bottom right of the screen:</p>
  <div class="image-adjacent">
    <p><img class="thumbnail border left"  src="{{ '/assets/images/word/Pages and Views/Print layout ribbon.png' | url }}" alt="Print layout view on ribbon in Word"  style="max-width:244px; width:100%; height:auto; vertical-align:middle"></p>

  <h4 id="read-mode-int">Read mode</h4>
  <p>Read mode is set up to allow content to be read-only. Content cannot be edited in read mode and the <span class="glossary-hover" data-term="ribbon">ribbon</span> is hidden with few view-only options provided like page colour, column width and layout that only apply to the Read mode view. There are view-only tools as well: <span class="glossary-hover" data-term="find">Find</span>, Navigation pane and Show Comments (Note: you can't add comments only view them). </p>
  <p><img class="thumbnail border"  src="{{ '/assets/images/word/Pages and Views/Read mode.png' | url }}" alt="Read mode view in Microsoft Word"  style="max-width:700px; width:100%; height:auto; vertical-align:middle"></p>
  <p>To exit Read mode either press <strong>Esc</strong>, select <strong>View > Edit Document</strong> or click the Print Layout button on the <span class="glossary-hover" data-term="status-bar">status bar</span> bottom right.</p>
  <div class="image-adjacent">
    <img class="thumbnail border left"  src="{{ '/assets/images/word/Pages and Views/Read mode exit.png' | url }}" alt="Read mode view in Microsoft Word"  style="max-width:350px; width:100%; height:auto; vertical-align:middle">
    <img class="thumbnail border left"  src="{{ '/assets/images/word/Pages and Views/Print layout status bar.png' | url }}" alt="Print layout view on status bar"  style="max-width:397px; width:100%; height:auto; vertical-align:middle">

  <h4 id="outline-view-int">Outline view</h4>
  <p>Outline view displays the document's content in a structured, hierarchical layout based on <span class="glossary-hover" data-term="outline-level">outline levels</span>. Layout elements such as <span class="glossary-hover" data-term="margin">margins</span>, <span class="glossary-hover" data-term="header">headers</span> and <span class="glossary-hover" data-term="footer">footers</span>, <span class="glossary-hover" data-term="page-break">page breaks</span> and images are hidden in order to focus on structural editing of the text. You can collapse and expand sections, promote and demote headings, and drag sections to reorder them.</p>
  <p>This is especially useful to fix headings if their <span class="glossary-hover" data-term="outline-level">outline levels</span> are out of place or inconsistent in a document and it is affecting how your <a href="/word/table-of-contents">table of contents</a> appears.</p>
  <p><img class="thumbnail border"  src="{{ '/assets/images/word/Pages and Views/Outline view.png' | url }}" alt="Outline view in Microsoft Word"  style="max-width:700px; width:100%; height:auto; vertical-align:middle"></p>
  <p>Turn on outline view on the ribbon: <strong>View > Views > Outline</strong></p>
  <p><img class="thumbnail border"  src="{{ '/assets/images/word/Pages and Views/Outline view ribbon.png' | url }}" alt="Outline view in Microsoft Word"  style="max-width:240px; width:100%; height:auto; vertical-align:middle"></p>
  <p>To exit outline view, click the <strong>Close Outline View</strong> button on the ribbon or press the Print layout button on the <span class="glossary-hover" data-term="status-bar">status bar</span> bottom right.</p>
  <div class="image-adjacent">
    <img class="thumbnail border left"  src="{{ '/assets/images/word/Pages and Views/Outline view close.png' | url }}" alt="Read mode view in Microsoft Word"  style="max-width:151px; width:100%; height:auto; vertical-align:middle">

  </div>

  <h4 id="draft-view-int">Draft view</h4>
  <p>Draft view displays the document's content in a simplified, text-focused layout. It hides most of the layout elements visible in the default <a href="#print-layout">Print layout</a> view such as<span class="glossary-hover" data-term="margin">margins</span>, <span class="glossary-hover" data-term="header">headers</span> and <span class="glossary-hover" data-term="footer">footers</span>, <span class="glossary-hover" data-term="page-break">page breaks</span> and images. Unlike <a href="#read-mode">Read mode</a>, Draft view allows editing, so it can be a useful tool for quickly writing and editing text without having to consider layout.</p>
  <p><img class="thumbnail border"  src="{{ '/assets/images/word/Pages and Views/Draft view.png' | url }}" alt="Draft view in Microsoft Word"  style="max-width:700px; width:100%; height:auto; vertical-align:middle"></p>

  <h4 id="web-layout-int">Web layout</h4>
  <p>Web layout is one you're unlikely to use. In the late 90s and early 2000s, Microsoft tried to make Word a WYSIWYG HTML editor allowing you to build webpages in Word. It was a terrible idea but people still did it. The web layout view was created to preview document pages as if they were webpages in a browser, i.e. with a continuous, fluid layout rather than fixed page boundaries, <span class="glossary-hover" data-term="margin">margins</span>, or <span class="glossary-hover" data-term="page-break">page breaks</span> that you get with the default <a href="#print-layout">Print layout</a> view. Text and images automatically reflow to fill the available width.</p>
  <p>The option is kept available today as some organisations still use Word for intranet content, but it also gives a view with continuous scrolling and has accessibility applications.</p>
  <p><img class="thumbnail border"  src="{{ '/assets/images/word/Pages and Views/Web layout.png' | url }}" alt="Web layout view in Microsoft Word"  style="max-width:700px; width:100%; height:auto; vertical-align:middle"></p>
  <p>Access web layout via the ribbon <strong>View > Views > Web layout</strong> </p>
  <div class="image-adjacent">
    <img class="thumbnail border left"  src="{{ '/assets/images/word/Pages and Views/Web layout ribbon.png' | url }}" alt="Read mode view in Microsoft Word"  style="max-width:240px; width:100%; height:auto; vertical-align:middle">
    <img class="thumbnail border left"  src="{{ '/assets/images/word/Pages and Views/Web layout status bar.png' | url }}" alt="Print layout view on status bar"  style="max-width:374px; width:100%; height:auto; vertical-align:middle">
  </div>
  <p>To exit web layout and return to the default view, on the ribbon click <strong>View > Views > Print Layout</strong>  or click the Print layout button on the <span class="glossary-hover" data-term="status-bar">status bar</span> bottom right.</p>

  <h2 id="layout-tab-int">Layout tab</h2>
  <p>Under <strong>Layout</strong> you can change the <a href="#size-int"><strong>size</strong></a> and <a href="#orientation-int"><strong>orientation</strong></a> of a page. You can also alter <a href="#margins-int"><strong>margins</strong></a>, <a href="#columns-int"><strong>columns</strong></a> and other specific layout options like <a href="#breaks-int"><strong>breaks</strong></a>, <a href="#hyphenation-int"><strong>hyphenation</strong></a> and <a href="#line-numbers-int"><strong>line numbers</strong></a>.</p>

  <p><img class="thumbnail border" src="{{ '/assets/images/word/Pages and Views/Layout Page Setup.png' | url }}"  alt="Layout tab options on ribbon in Word" style="max-width:375px; width:100%; height:auto; vertical-align:middle"></p>

  <h3 id="margins-int">Margins</h3>
  <p>The page margins determine the space between the edge of the page and the editable area. The options on this menu let you decide how narrow or wide. You can set your own via <strong>Custom Margins...</strong> at the bottom, this is explored in this article: <a href="/word/page-setup-options">Page setup options</a></p>

  <h3 id="orientation-int">Orientation</h3>
  <p>Changing from portrait to landscape has its benefits where required, for example when your table needs more horizontal space for columns or placing large landscape images. Headers and footers still apply but will be longer and sometimes you may need to adjust content with <span class="glossary-hover" data-term="indent-markers">indent markers</span> which are explored in this article: <a href="/word/indents/">Indents</a>.</p>

  <p><img class="thumbnail border left" src="{{ '/assets/images/word/Pages and Views/Orientation.png' | url }}"  alt="Page orientation options in Word" style="max-width:146px; width:100%; height:auto; vertical-align:middle"></p>

  <h4>What if I want a mix of portrait and landscape?</h4>
  <p>To split the orientation of pages between portrait and landscape you'll need to use <span class="glossary-hover" data-term="section-break">section breaks</span>. This is demonstrated in the <a href="/word/section-breaks/">Section breaks</a> article.</p>

 <h3 id="size-int">Size</h3>

  <h3 id="hyphenation-int">Hyphenation</h3>
  <p>By default Word disables hyphenation for ease of reading. The Hyphenation menu gives you a few options to choose from. <u>Note</u>: Normally for hyphenation to work you'd need to set the text alignment to <strong>Justified</strong> first.</p>

  <p><img class="thumbnail left border" src="{{ '/assets/images/word/Pages and Views/Hyphenation menu.png' | url }}"  alt="Hyphenation menu in Word" style="max-width:200px; width:100%; height:auto; vertical-align:middle"></p>

  <p>It's set to <strong>None</strong> by default, but if you select <strong>Automatic</strong> then Word applies hyphenation to the whole document, here's an example:</p>

  <p><img class="thumbnail border left" src="{{ '/assets/images/word/Pages and Views/Hyphenation example.png' | url }}"  alt="Page Orientation and Size options in Word" style="max-width:650px; width:100%; height:auto; vertical-align:middle"></p>

  <p>If you select <strong>Manual</strong>, Word gives you the option of exactly where to hyphenate a word more specifically:</p>

  <p><img class="thumbnail border left" src="{{ '/assets/images/word/Pages and Views/Hyphenation manual.png' | url }}"  alt="Page Orientation and Size options in Word" style="max-width:650px; width:100%; height:auto; vertical-align:middle"></p>

  <p>Select <strong>Hyphenation options</strong> and you can determine some greater control.</p>
  <p><img class="thumbnail border left" src="{{ '/assets/images/word/Pages and Views/Hyphenation options.png' | url }}"  alt="Page Orientation and Size options in Word" style="max-width:275px; width:100%; height:auto; vertical-align:middle"></p>
  <ul>
    <li><u>Hyphenate words in CAPS</u> (all capitals)</li>
    <li><u>Limit consecutive hyphens to</u> a set number — this prevents too many lines in a row from ending with a hyphenated word. E.g. If you select '2' Word won't allow more than 2 consecutive lines to end with a hyphen. If a third line would need one, Word adjusts spacing instead.</li>
    <li><u>Hyphenation zone</u>: Ignore this one, it's a legacy option from Word 2010 and earlier. This is to do with the amount of white space allowed between the end of the last word and the right margin before a hyphen was inserted. Modern versions of Word manage this automatically.</li>
  </ul>
